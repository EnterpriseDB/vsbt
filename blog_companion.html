Random vs Sequential: What I Learned Benchmarking pgvector and VectorChord at a Billion Vectors

By Alessandro Ferraresi, Principal Database Engineer at EnterpriseDB · April 2026

Vector search is moving from a side workload to the critical path of agentic applications. When an agent needs to retrieve grounding context for a decision — on every step of a multi-step plan, for thousands of concurrent users — the index behind that retrieval becomes a real performance ceiling. So I set out to answer a simple, expensive question: which PostgreSQL vector index actually holds up under enterprise scale? I benchmarked pgvector’s HNSW index against VectorChord’s IVF‑RaBitQ index across 5 million, 100 million, and 1 billion vectors, on the same hardware, at matched recall. This post is the short version of what I found, why it happens, and how I’d advise teams to choose between the two.

The question I wanted to settle

pgvector is an open-source Postgres extension; its HNSW index is a multi-layer proximity graph for approximate nearest-neighbor (ANN) search. Modern embedding pipelines routinely cross 2,000 dimensions, and datasets are growing past hundreds of millions of vectors. Agentic applications also generate concurrency profiles closer to OLTP than to traditional ANN demos: an agent loop fans out many vector lookups per step, and a fleet of agents serving end users runs many loops in parallel. My hypothesis: HNSW’s random‑I/O access pattern stops scaling well before the workload outgrows a reasonable server, and an index built for sequential reads pulls ahead long before any architectural ceiling. I ran pgvector head-to-head against VectorChord, another Postgres extension, implementing IVF‑RaBitQ.

How I tested

A single AWS i7i.metal-48xl (96 cores, 1.5 TB RAM, 13.6 TiB striped NVMe) running Postgres 17.9 with pgvector 0.8.2 and VectorChord 1.0.0. Three datasets: LAION 5M and 100M (768-dim, inner product), Deep1B (96-dim, L2). I swept search parameters and compared the points closest to ~0.95 recall, at 1 and 32 clients. To isolate memory pressure as the variable, I used Linux huge pages to constrain the OS page cache, sweeping available RAM from 8 GB up to the full 1.5 TB. CPU and NVMe were not scaled down, so the results describe performance as a function of RAM on this hardware, not performance on smaller cloud instances where CPU and storage would also be reduced. Everything ran through an open-source benchmark suite my team maintains, the Vector Search Benchmark Tool.

Finding 1: VectorChord builds indexes 5–12× faster, and the gap widens

At 5 million vectors, both engines build in minutes. At a billion vectors, the difference is a workday: pgvector took 9.4 hours; VectorChord took 46 minutes. HNSW has to search the existing graph for every vector inserted; IVF‑RaBitQ’s k‑means and quantization passes scale predictably.

Build memory works differently too. pgvector’s HNSW must keep the entire graph in maintenance_work_mem — I set that to 1 TB for the 1B build, and if it spills, pgvector switches to a much slower on-disk mode. VectorChord doesn’t use maintenance_work_mem; clustering buffers are allocated by the Rust allocator instead.

Finding 2: Throughput at matched recall, across memory tiers

Across every dataset and every memory tier, VectorChord meets or beats pgvector HNSW — and where memory is tight relative to the index, the gap becomes an order of magnitude. At 100M vectors with 16 GB of shared_buffers and 64 GB of available RAM (indexes 367–405 GB), VectorChord serves 4,644 QPS under 32 clients; pgvector serves 377. That’s a 12.3× gap, not a tuning issue. At 5M, where the index fits comfortably in RAM, VectorChord is still 1.4× ahead. At 1B with abundant memory, VectorChord wins concurrent throughput by 55% with half the P99 latency.

The other thing the chart shows is how flat VectorChord’s curves are. From 16 GB to 512 GB of shared_buffers, its 100M throughput stays between 4,123 and 4,644 QPS — a 13% spread. pgvector’s ranges from 377 to 1,917 over the same range, a 5× spread. At 1B, pgvector even peaks at 256 GB of shared_buffers and declines beyond that.

Finding 3: At a billion vectors, single-client behavior is just as different

With abundant RAM, single-client QPS converges: pgvector hits 36 QPS at 1 TB of available RAM, VectorChord hits 36 at 512 GB. By that measure they look equivalent. At the 128 GB available-RAM tier (against a 469–646 GB index), pgvector falls to 1.5 QPS with a 683 ms median latency; VectorChord still serves 31 QPS at 29 ms. A 20× single-client gap is the kind of operational difference that turns a working deployment into a paged-on-call incident. Under 32 concurrent clients at the same memory, pg_stat_activity shows every pgvector backend blocked on BufferIo waits — CPUs idle while NVMe is saturated with random reads.

Why this happens: it’s the storage hierarchy

After running the full sweep, every result reduces to one architectural property: HNSW does random I/O; IVF does sequential I/O. Concurrency scaling, memory sensitivity, tail latency, build cost — all of it falls out of that single difference.

pgvector’s HNSW is a multi-layer proximity graph. At ef_search=380 on the 1B index, a query visits roughly 380 nodes, and graph neighbors are chosen by vector similarity, not physical proximity — so consecutive hops almost never sit on the same page. The OS prefetcher cannot help; each hop is an independent random 8 KB read.

VectorChord builds a two-level k‑means tree. A query routes to a handful of coarse centroids (resident in memory), descends to ~250 fine lists out of 640,000, and scans them. Each list is contiguous on disk, so the OS prefetcher and NVMe controller serve them as large sequential reads at GB/s. Vectors are RaBitQ-quantized to 1 bit per dimension; SIMD distance computation chews through them at memory bandwidth, and a reranking pass over the top candidates uses full-precision vectors stored alongside.

Both engines read a similar volume of data per query. VectorChord just reads it 6–10× faster — and that’s the whole report in one sentence.

The cascade is straightforward: random reads bottleneck on storage queue depth long before CPU saturates, the working set is effectively the whole index (no spatial locality), variable graph paths produce long-tail outliers, and the build phase pays the same random‑I/O tax. The pgvector decline past 256 GB of shared_buffers at 1B is a secondary effect — the classic double-buffering penalty from holding the same pages in both shared_buffers and the OS page cache — covered in the full report.

So when do you pick what?

pgvector HNSW is the right pick when your dataset fits comfortably in RAM (~5M and below in our tests) with light concurrency, or when you need very high recall and are willing to trade QPS to get there — HNSW exposes ef_search and M as recall/QPS knobs.

Reach for VectorChord when the index doesn’t fit in RAM (or might not always), when you serve concurrent traffic, when you rebuild on a regular cadence, when you need predictable tail latency under load (we observed 50–75% tighter P99s), or when your embedding model produces >2,000 dimensions (pgvector’s 8 KB page caps HNSW around there).

The honest summary: at small scale and light load, pgvector HNSW is fine. At everything past that — bigger datasets, real concurrency, tighter memory budgets, faster rebuilds — VectorChord wins decisively, and the gap grows with scale. The good news for Postgres users is that you don’t have to pick one forever. Both extensions run on the same database, the same SQL, the same operational tooling. EDB Postgres AI ships both and lets teams choose per workload, and migrate as their requirements evolve.
